# AAA Menu / HUD Design Research for Dragon Drift
(Produced by a web-research agent, 2026-07-14. Sources cited at bottom.)

## (a) The patterns that define "AAA premium" — 15 named patterns

**1. The Hero Scene menu (live world, not a wallpaper)**
Ghost of Tsushima's title screen is the canonical example: a single katana planted in a grass field, wind and storm particles moving through the live scene, and the scene *changes with story progress* (sword only → two swords → Ghost mask). Forza Horizon 5 idles a car in the live world with time-of-day; Gran Turismo 7 puts real car models in a slowly-orbiting camera scene behind its menus; Cyberpunk 2077's 2.0 menu is V in a rendered scene. Dragon Drift already does this — the differentiators to copy are: (1) the scene must have *idle life* (wind, drifting particles, slow camera dolly at ~1–2°/sec, the dragon breathing/shifting weight), (2) the scene should *react to menu state* (selecting a dragon = camera moves to frame it; entering settings = camera drifts, background defocuses), and (3) the scene should *reflect progression* (last biome flown, current dragon). DOM translation: drive a slow procedural camera path in Three.js while the menu is open; treat every menu screen as a *camera shot*, not a page.

**2. One typeface, huge scale contrast, tight discipline**
The premium look almost never comes from a fancy font — it comes from scale discipline. God of War/Elden Ring/Destiny use one or two faces total; the criticism of AAA menus ("flat buttons, small sans-serif text, tabs") shows the floor, and games escape it via *scale contrast*: a very large, letterspaced display line (screen title) against small, quiet body text — nothing in between. Elden Ring: high-contrast engraved serif (Mantinia/Agmena-based) for the identity layer, neutral face for system text. FH5 redesigns pair a wide extra-bold display with a technical body face. **Translation with no font files:** use the system stack but *work it*: `font-weight: 200–300` at large sizes (SF/Roboto/Segoe all ship light weights), `text-transform: uppercase` + `letter-spacing: 0.15–0.35em` for display lines and labels, `font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums` for scores/speeds, and a strict 3-tier type scale (e.g. 11–12px caps labels / 15–16px body / 34–48px display). Letterspaced light-weight all-caps is *the* single cheapest "AAA" signal available in pure CSS.

**3. Hairline everything (the 1px aesthetic)**
Destiny 2, Elden Ring, Cyberpunk, and virtually all sci-fi HUDs use 1px lines as the primary structural element: 1px separators instead of boxes, 1px borders on panels (Elden Ring's gold hairline frame on dark translucent panels), thin ring/arc meters instead of chunky bars. Glassmorphism guides converge on the same recipe: translucent panel + "thin white or semi-transparent border" + subtle inner glow. Translation: `border: 1px solid rgba(255,255,255,0.14)` (hairline, low alpha, never pure white), plus a 1px inner top-edge highlight (`inset 0 1px 0 rgba(255,255,255,0.08)`). Never rounded-corner radius > ~8px on a "serious" panel; Genshin/HSR use 2–6px.

**4. Dark translucent panel over live world, world stays legible**
The universal AAA pause/menu treatment (GoW Ragnarök, Horizon FW, Tsushima, Genshin): a dark scrim (rgba(0,0,0,0.4–0.7)) or gradient scrim over the live 3D scene, with the world still visible and *moving* behind it. Elden Ring keeps the world fully live behind its map/menus. Two premium upgrades: (1) defocus the world when a menu opens — in Three.js do it cheaply by rendering at reduced resolution or lerping a fog/exposure change rather than a real DoF pass; (2) *dim asymmetrically* — gradient scrim heavier behind the text column, lighter where the hero (dragon) is framed.

**5. Menu = camera move (transitions travel through the world)**
Premium games transition between screens by moving the camera, not by swapping DOM. GT7 and FH5 fly the camera to a new vantage; Destiny's Director zooms between orbit/planet; Persona 5 masks screen changes with character-driven wipes. For Dragon Drift: main→hangar = camera dolly to the dragon; hangar→biome select = pull up above the world. DOM panels then only need to do a cheap fade/slide *synchronized with* the camera move — the camera does the expensive-feeling work for free.

**6. The two-step entry ritual ("Press any button" → menu)**
Nearly every AAA title (GoW, Elden Ring, Tsushima, AC7) separates the *title moment* (logo + hero scene + one prompt line, lots of negative space) from the *functional menu* (list appears after input, usually with a staggered reveal and an audio sting). This is pure theater and costs nothing: title state shows only the logotype and a pulsing "TAP TO BEGIN" (opacity 0.5↔1.0, ~1.8s cycle); on tap, logo scales down/rises, menu items stagger in.

**7. Left-rail list, right-side hero (the AAA layout grid)**
The dominant menu grid (GoW Ragnarök, Horizon, Tsushima, Cyberpunk, AC Shadows): vertical text list anchored left (aligned to a consistent margin, ~5–8% inset), the right two-thirds left EMPTY to show the hero scene. Souls games deliberately put battle-relevant info left, secondary right, following left-to-right reading hierarchy. Negative space *is* the luxury signal — a premium menu is ~60–70% empty. Selected item treatment: not a filled button, but a hairline underline/left-tick + slight indent (4–8px translateX) + brightness bump.

**8. Hover/focus states with physicality (the Destiny standard)**
Destiny's award-winning UI built everything on a free cursor with *rich hover states*: items brighten, scale ~1.02–1.05, grow a hairline outline, and show a tooltip after a beat — detail lives in hover, keeping the resting UI clean. Destiny also popularized *hold-to-confirm* (circular progress ring filling over ~1s) for destructive/committal actions — excellent on touch (no accidental taps, feels weighty). Translation: every interactive element needs 4 visible states (rest / pressed / selected / disabled); on touch, "hover" becomes the pressed state — scale to 0.97 + brighten on `touchstart` within 50ms.

**9. Withheld color (one accent, earned)**
Premium palettes are near-monochrome (white/off-white text, ~60% gray secondary, dark scrims) with ONE saturated accent used only for meaning: Destiny's per-rarity colors, Elden Ring's gold, Cyberpunk's cyan/red, ZZZ's acid yellow-green. Dragon Drift should pick one accent per biome/dragon (which also ties menu → world) and forbid it everywhere except: selection, boost/special-ready, and danger (a second reserved red). Everything else grayscale + alpha. This also solves HDR-ish pop cheaply: accent at full saturation reads "glowing" against a desaturated UI.

**10. Diegetic where it's cheap, overlay where it matters**
Dead Space's RIG (health as glowing spine segments on the character model; "diegetic by implementation" — Dino Ignacio, GDC) and Squadrons' cockpit instruments (every readout exists on the ship; hardcore mode removes the overlay entirely) are the immersion gold standard. For a third-person dragon game, full diegesis is wrong, but *partial* diegesis is free and premium: dragon health/boost as component glow on the dragon itself (Dragon Drift already has "withheld component glow" in its dragon kit — boost charge as building glow along the wing membranes or tail is exactly the Dead Space pattern), leaving the DOM HUD for score/objective only. Squadrons' other lesson: let players toggle HUD layers off — an "immersive HUD" option is both an accessibility feature and a screenshot generator.

**11. The Hoyoverse mobile package (Genshin / HSR / ZZZ)**
The most-studied premium mobile UI family. Concrete takeaways: Genshin keeps exploration HUD minimal (top-left minimap, bottom-right thumb-arc cluster of attack/skill/burst buttons sized for thumbs, everything else hidden until relevant) with large touch targets and color-coded feedback; menus are full-screen with translucent layering and strong typographic hierarchy. HSR fixes Genshin's density problem with a left-edge vertical nav rail, generous padding, and consistent card grammar; its banner/menu animations use "impactful big shapes plus secondary animation of small dots" — one big motion + tiny secondary particles = finish. ZZZ's stated philosophy (official dev notes): "high-quality visual packaging with minimal animated transitions" on high-frequency screens — flashy motion is spent on rare screens (character reveal), while daily-use screens snap fast. Budget rule for Dragon Drift: spend motion on menu entry and dragon selection; make settings/inventory instant.

**12. Auto-hiding, context-sensitive HUD (Sky / Tsushima)**
Sky: Children of the Light (built mobile-first by thatgamecompany) fades nearly all UI when you're just flying; controls reappear on touch. Ghost of Tsushima's "guiding wind" replaced the nav marker with a world effect. Alto's Odyssey shows only a score line; Monument Valley has effectively zero HUD. Pattern: every HUD element gets a *relevance condition* and fades to 0 (or to 20% ghost) within ~2s of being irrelevant — health hidden at full health, score fades during calm cruise, everything returns instantly (150ms) on damage/pickup/boss. Minimal-by-default is both the premium mobile look and a straight FPS win (fewer composited layers).

**13. Everything animates in, nothing pops**
In Hades (whose UI/VFX artist has been doing "flashy, snappy, crispy" menu motion since Transistor), every panel, button and title enters with motion — slide + fade + slight overshoot — and leaves faster than it entered. Staggered reveal of list items (top to bottom, 30–60ms apart) is the signature AAA menu tell (GoW, Horizon, Cyberpunk all do it). A DOM menu where elements just appear reads instantly as amateur.

**14. Audio is half the premium feel**
Consistent finding: hover/move ticks, select confirms, back sounds, and an "open menu" whoosh give instant input confirmation and are mapped in an attention hierarchy (critical > confirm > hover > ambient). Nintendo Switch's crisp click is the reference for "expensive." Dragon Drift is procedural-audio-friendly: short WebAudio blips (~30–80ms, filtered noise + sine at 2–4kHz for ticks, lower pitch for back/cancel, small upward interval for confirm) synthesized at runtime cost nothing and are the single highest ROI polish item after motion.

**15. Accessibility as a premium signal**
The 2024+ AAA baseline (AC Shadows: 20+ options; DOOM TDA, MH Wilds): three colorblind filter presets (deuteranopia/protanopia/tritanopia), HUD scale slider (0.75–1.5×), per-element HUD opacity/visibility toggles, text size options, reduced-motion toggle. Shipping even a compact version (HUD scale, HUD opacity, colorblind-safe accent alternates, reduce motion) reads as AAA care and is nearly free in a CSS-variable-driven UI (`--hud-scale`, `--hud-alpha`, `--accent`).

## (b) In-flight HUD for a fast dragon game

**Layout: center is sacred.** At speed the eye locks to the vanishing point. AC7 and Squadrons keep the center reserved for reticle + target info only; persistent meters live at edges/corners. Souls-style: player-critical info bottom/left, contextual info near center only when actionable.

**Reticle & lock-on (rail-shooter lineage — Star Fox, Panzer Dragoon, Rez):**
- Two-part reticle: a near cursor + a far "lead" indicator on the flight vector (Star Fox's dual reticle) — communicates where you're going vs. where you'll hit.
- Lock-on grammar: box/diamond appears on target → *shrinks onto and snaps to* the target while flashing (Star Fox's charge cursor flashes red when locked; AC7 plays a distinct lock tone; its Dynamic Launch Zone shows a range bar with a caret so you know lock range). The premium tells are the *snap animation* (shrink from 3× to 1× over ~150ms, ease-out) and a *distinct audio state change* (searching tick → solid lock tone).
- Multi-lock: Rez/Panzer Dragoon lock up to 8 targets with numbered/chained markers, released as one salvo — extremely satisfying for a dragon breath-weapon; render lock markers as DOM divs projected from 3D positions (one transform update per frame, `will-change: transform`).
- Off-screen targets: edge arrows/chevrons that slide along the screen border (AC7 convention), fading with distance.

**Speed and boost feedback — mostly NOT in the HUD.** The research consensus for speed feel: FOV widening on boost (simplest, most effective), speed lines/particles scaling with velocity, camera shake, wind audio swell, slight vignette. The HUD's role is small: a thin boost arc/ring near the reticle (not a corner bar — you can't look away at speed), which lives near the center only while boosting. Dragon-native: boost charge as wing-glow (pattern 10) with the HUD arc as a redundant precise readout.

**Damage feedback:** the standard taxonomy: directional arc segments around screen center (up = ahead, down = behind) + red edge vignette for severity — edge vignette is "felt more than seen," which is right for a game where you can't read UI at speed. Indicator size scales with hit strength (Overwatch convention). Duration ~0.6–1.0s fade. Add a 40–80ms world flash on the dragon (hit-stop is risky at speed; skip it).

**Player health:** thin arc or segmented pips bottom-center or bottom-left, hidden at full health (pattern 12); pulse at ≤30%; screen-edge red creep at critical — never a fat bar.

**Boss health bars:** two valid conventions — (1) Elden Ring style: single hairline-thin bottom-center bar, boss name in display type above, damage shown as a white/yellow "recently lost" chunk that drains after a beat (that lag chunk is the single most copied juice detail in boss bars); (2) segmented/phase bars (multiple pips or notch marks) telegraphing phase transitions. Monster Hunter famously shows *no* boss health at all (part flinches/limping = health). For Dragon Drift bosses: Elden Ring placement + drain-lag, with phase notches on the bar, name + epithet in the letterspaced display style, and the bar's intro animated (fills left-to-right over ~800ms as the boss title card shows) — the boss *title card* (name reveal, 1.5–2s, large type, world briefly graded) is itself a core AAA convention (Souls, Tsushima duels, MH).

**Score/combo:** top-right, tabular numerals, fades to ~30% during calm; on gain, a small count-up (number ticks, ~300ms) + 1.06 scale pop, never particles-everywhere.

## (c) Motion design numbers (what premium actually uses)

- **Durations:** micro-interactions (press, hover, toggle) 120–200ms; panel/modal in 250–350ms; panel out 150–250ms (exits always faster than entries); full-screen transitions 400–600ms; anything over ~700ms without a camera move feels sluggish.
- **Easing:** entries `cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1)` (strong ease-out, "settles"); exits ease-in; overshoot for selection/confirm only — `cubic-bezier(0.34, 1.56, 0.64, 1)` (≈5–10% overshoot) or spring physics for things that respond to a gesture. Never `linear`, never default `ease` on hero motion. Define 2–3 named easings and reuse them everywhere — motion consistency is itself the language.
- **Stagger:** 40–120ms between siblings; 30–50ms for menu lists; cap individually-staggered items at ~10–15 (beyond that, animate as a group/wave). Direction should follow reading order or the camera move.
- **Button press juice:** scale 0.96–0.97 + brighten on down (≤50ms response), release with overshoot back to 1.0, plus tick sound; selected item gets accent underline growing from 0 to full width (~200ms).
- **The HSR finish detail:** one big primary motion + tiny secondary elements (dots, hairline sweeps, a 1px shine sweep across a panel) trailing 60–100ms behind. Secondary motion is what separates "animated" from "crafted."
- **Number changes:** always tween (count-up ~300–500ms, ease-out) — instant number swaps read cheap.
- **Idle motion:** the menu must never be fully still — prompt pulse (~1.8s sine), scene drift, occasional 1px shine sweep every ~8–12s. Subtlety rule: if you notice the idle loop, it's too strong.
- **Reduced motion:** honor `prefers-reduced-motion` by collapsing to fades.

## (d) Mobile/perf caveats (weak GPU, DOM over WebGL)

- **`backdrop-filter` is the big trap.** Real frosted glass forces the browser to re-blur the composited canvas *every frame* while the 3D scene animates behind it. Guidance: mobile tolerates ~3–5 small blurred elements at rest; on a page with a full-screen animating WebGL canvas behind it, treat the budget as **one** panel with `blur(≤8px)`, and prefer **zero during gameplay**. Premium-looking cheap substitute: dark translucent fill (rgba(10,14,20,0.55–0.75)) + hairline border + 1px inner top highlight + very subtle CSS gradient "sheen" — reads as glass without sampling the backdrop. Or fake the frost in-engine: since the menu owns the camera, render the scene slightly defocused/darker while menus are open (you're paying for the render anyway). Always wrap real blur in `@supports (backdrop-filter: blur(1px))` with the tinted fallback, and gate it behind your quality-tier detection.
- **Animate only `transform` and `opacity`.** Never animate width/height/top/left/box-shadow/filter on HUD elements; those trigger layout/paint next to a 60fps canvas. `will-change: transform` on the handful of per-frame-updated elements (lock markers, damage arcs), and *remove* it from everything else — every `will-change`/3D-transformed element is another composited layer competing with the WebGL surface for GPU memory on weak devices.
- **Don't touch the DOM per frame except transforms.** Projected markers: update `style.transform = translate3d(...)` only; batch text updates (score) to when the value changes; avoid per-frame class toggles.
- **Cheap depth without shadows:** big soft `box-shadow` on many elements is a paint cost; prefer scrim gradients + hairlines. One shadow on the single active modal is fine.
- **SVG/canvas for procedural chrome:** inline SVG (data-URI or DOM) gives you arcs, reticles, ring meters, and ornamental corner brackets with zero asset files; a ring meter is one SVG circle with animated `stroke-dashoffset` (composited cheaply). Avoid SVG filters (feGaussianBlur etc.) on mobile — paint cost similar to backdrop-filter.
- **Safe areas & touch:** `viewport-fit=cover` + `env(safe-area-inset-*)` padding on all HUD anchors; nothing tappable within ~16pt of screen left/right edges (system gesture conflict); touch targets ≥44pt (48dp), and remember spacing between targets matters more than size above ~40pt. Keep in-flight touch controls inside the natural thumb arcs (bottom corners, Genshin's cluster), never top-center.
- **Text rendering:** avoid `text-shadow` glows on frequently-updated text; use `font-smoothing` defaults; letterspaced caps at small sizes need ≥11px to stay legible on 1x-ish cheap screens.

## (e) Anti-patterns (what reads cheap/amateur)

1. Elements that pop in/out with no transition, or all animating with default `ease`/`linear`.
2. Filled rounded-rectangle buttons with heavy borders and drop shadows ("Bootstrap in space") — AAA selection is typographic + hairline, not button-chrome.
3. More than one saturated color family; rainbow rarity colors everywhere; pure #FFF text on pure #000 panels (use off-white ~92% and near-black blue-gray).
4. Center-stacked everything / no alignment grid; text not sharing a common left margin.
5. Fat opaque health bars, chunky bevels, faux-gold ornament borders done as CSS gradients — instant asset-flip look. Without image assets, don't attempt ornament — go minimal instead.
6. Overlong transitions (>600ms) or unskippable animation between the player and play; flashy motion on high-frequency screens (ZZZ's rule: spend flash on rare screens).
7. HUD always at full opacity showing everything at once; tutorial text that never leaves; more than one message/toast at a time (GoW Ragnarök used a single-slot message queue for exactly this).
8. Dead silence on input, or a single reused blip for hover/confirm/cancel alike.
9. Text under ~11px, low-contrast gray-on-glass body copy (Cyberpunk's most-cited flaw), hit targets under 44pt, UI under the notch.
10. Real backdrop-blur everywhere and janky 40fps menus — a fluid fake beats a stuttering real effect; the frame rate *is* the premium feel.
11. Instant number swaps, progress bars that jump, damage with no directional cue.
12. Idle-dead menus: a static screenshot-like menu over a frozen world reads like a template; the world behind must live.
